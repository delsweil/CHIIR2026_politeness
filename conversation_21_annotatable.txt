why does the duck skin need to be pricked?
[conversation_id=EioCaKa] [conversation.id=-NGzu-_WzJn8QoBmrlTy] [conversation.participant=user]

[conversation_id=EioCaKa] [conversation.id=-NGzu1dzit4-PafnK1bp] [conversation.participant=agent]

Duck is high in fat, so when roasting a whole duck you can end up with a lot of fat left with the duck inside the skin and soggier skin. This can present a challenge when cooking, because we want the fat to render out and the skin to become crispy and delicious.The most common way to do this, classically, is to prick the skin all over, to permit the fat an exit path (when doing this, be careful to cut through the skin into the fat layer, but hopefully not into the meat itself)

what is the origin of this recipe?
[conversation_id=EioCaKa] [conversation.id=-NGzwKPmmeThNCECZtlO] [conversation.participant=user]

Duck à l'orange, or canard à l'orange in French, is a classic French dish in which a duck is roasted and served with a bigarade sauce. The Italians claim it migrated from Italy to France. Italian poet Antonio Caccitore detailed his first encounter with duck à l'orange outside of Italy in the poem "Anatra all'Arancia", which tells the story of how the dish made its way to Paris from its origin in Naples.
